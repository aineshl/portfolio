The Problem

The existing explosion-proof offering ran on a white-label product. The structural problem: we couldn't compete on price, we couldn't differentiate on features, and we had no supply chain control. When the supplier was acquired by a competitor, lead times doubled and the entire product line became a business risk overnight.

The answer wasn't to find a better white-label. It was to build one we owned.

Discovery First

Before defining a single feature, I ran 10–15 structured customer interviews across three market segments. The findings shaped everything:

Customers often spec explosion-proof for durability and "industrial look" — not just hazard classification. This reframed our positioning from spec-matching competitors toward building a platform designed to grow. We weren't just solving a certification problem; we were building a product that needed to belong in a harsh environment.

Other strong signals from discovery: remote calibration was considered mandatory by every participant; native BACnet was a genuine differentiator (virtually no competitor in our segment offered it); Bluetooth and cloud connectivity got consistent negative signals — both deliberately excluded from V1.

Product Design

I designed a platform, not a single SKU — built to support multiple configurations and expand into adjacent markets:

  • Up to 3 sensors (internal and/or remote) — reduces install cost, simplifies system design
  • Broad gas coverage: EC, IR, CAT, PID — wider than most competitors in the segment
  • Native BACnet and Modbus — a genuine differentiator at this price point
  • Remote calibration — validated as non-negotiable across all discovery participants
  • Built on existing firmware platform — simplifies service, training, and support from day one

A Key Tradeoff: Scoping Certification

After researching three certification labs and completing discovery, I cut ATEX from V1. It would have added significant cost, 14+ weeks, and ongoing ISO audit obligations — for minimal demand uplift in our primary target segment. The call protected the timeline, preserved budget for GTM investment, and let us build pipeline while certification was underway. This was a deliberate decision, not a constraint.

Business Case & Pricing

I built a full NPV model to evaluate in-house vs. white-label across multiple volume and pricing scenarios. The key findings that drove the go decision:

  • In-house manufacturing achieved ~71% per-unit cost reduction — restoring pricing competitiveness and margin control
  • Program NPV-positive within two years at conservative volume projections
  • Target price set to match the competitive benchmark identified in discovery
  • Pricing model supported both market-penetration and value-based approaches by channel

Go-to-Market

Three-tier sequencing to maximize speed-to-revenue without overextending:

  • Tier 1 — fast wins: Existing dealer network, established relationships, near-term pipeline (distilleries, fleet, boiler rooms)
  • Tier 2 — strategic expansion: Wastewater, chemical processing, midstream — longer cycles, new dealer development required
  • Tier 3 — long-term: Offshore and premium industrial; requires ATEX (planned for V2)

Executed across trade show roadshow, dealer briefing materials, sales training, and full technical documentation.

Results

  • Eliminated a high-risk white-label dependency — the company now owns the platform, the pricing, and the roadmap
  • ~71% per-unit cost reduction achieved through in-house design and manufacturing
  • Entered CSA/UL certification with validated demand and early pipeline already established
  • Platform architecture positions the company for expansion into adjacent markets
  • Differentiated through BACnet, multi-sensor capability, and remote calibration — features competitors in this segment largely don't offer
