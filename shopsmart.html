The problem

Amazon shopping is cognitively exhausting. Dozens of tabs open. Reviews that contradict each other. Star ratings that tell you nothing about sample size — a 4.3 with 40 reviews and a 4.3 with 14,000 reviews are presented identically, but they're not the same thing.

I started with surveys, not assumptions. 100+ responses revealed consistent patterns:

  • 72% of users spent too much time comparing Amazon products
  • 54% couldn't reliably interpret rating vs. review volume
  • 45% spent excessive time reading reviews just to figure out what to buy

Amazon had filters. It didn't have synthesis. That was the gap.

My role

Everything — sole PM, designer, and developer. User research, problem framing, MVP scoping, Figma prototyping, full-stack development, scoring algorithm design, AI integration, user testing, and iteration.

What I built

ShopSmart takes two Amazon product URLs and returns a side-by-side breakdown, a ShopSmart Score, and AI-generated summaries of product features and the 100 most recent reviews — so users can decide faster with more confidence.

  • ShopSmart Score — weighted algorithm combining price, rating, and review volume as a reliability proxy. Not just stars. Trustworthiness of those stars.
  • Recommended pick with rationale — users wanted a verdict, not a spreadsheet
  • AI feature summary — BART model synthesizing product description into key differentiators
  • AI review summary — BART model across the 100 most recent reviews, not cherry-picked highlights
  • Parallel processing — both products scraped simultaneously with a loading state that communicates progress

The build

Full-stack: Python/Flask backend handling scraping, data structuring, and AI inference; HTML/CSS/JS frontend. I designed the layouts in Figma before writing any code — then validated the flow with users before the build.

Stack: Python · Flask · JavaScript · HTML/CSS · BART model (Hugging Face) · Figma

Both products are scraped in parallel to cut load time, and the loading state was specifically designed after users flagged the wait as friction in the first round of testing.

What 15+ testers told me

Four themes came back consistently from user testing. Each one became a ship decision:

Slow processing. Fixed with parallel scraping and a loading state with dynamic progress indication. Perceived wait dropped significantly.

Rating and review signals lacked context. Added explanation of what the ShopSmart Score actually weights. Users don't trust opaque numbers — they trust explained numbers.

No recommendation. The most-requested feature — users wanted a verdict, not just a comparison table. Added the recommended pick with explicit rationale.

Visual design. Did a full redesign pass for readability and trust. Aesthetic credibility matters when you're asking users to trust an algorithm with a purchase decision.

What this demonstrates

ShopSmart is proof of the full PM loop: identify a real problem through research, not intuition; prototype before building; ship an MVP; get honest feedback; iterate on what actually matters; polish for trust.

The technical build — scraping, scoring, AI summarization, parallel processing — was secondary to the product thinking that shaped what to build and why. That's the sequence I care about getting right.
